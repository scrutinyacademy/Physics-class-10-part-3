Refraction of Light at Curved Surfaces

Lenses · Image Formation · Focal Length · Lens Formula · Magnification

Key Concepts
Core Definitions
  • Pole (P): Centre of the curved surface
  • Centre of Curvature (C): Centre of the sphere of which the surface is a part
  • Principal Axis: Line joining C and P
  • Focal Point (F): Where refracted rays converge or appear to diverge from
  • Focal Length (f): Distance between F and optic centre
  • Optic Centre: Midpoint of a thin lens
  • Paraxial Rays: Rays very close to the principal axis
Types of Lenses
  • Biconvex (Double Convex): Thick at middle, converging lens
  • Biconcave (Double Concave): Thin at middle, diverging lens
  • Plano-Convex / Plano-Concave: One flat, one curved surface
  • Concavo-Convex: Both curved in same direction
  • Air bubble in water acts as diverging lens
  • Convex lens in denser medium can act as diverging lens

Sign Convention & Ray Rules
  • All distances from pole/optic centre
  • Distances along incident ray → positive
  • Distances opposite to incident ray → negative
  • Heights above principal axis → positive
  • Ray through optic centre → undeviated
  • Ray parallel to axis → passes through F
  • Ray through F → emerges parallel to axis
Important Formulas
All Key Formulas
Curved Surface Formula
n₂/v − n₁/u = (n₂−n₁)/R
Refraction at a single curved interface
Lens Formula
1/f = 1/v − 1/u
f = focal length, v = image distance, u = object distance
Lens Maker's Formula
1/f = (n−1)(1/R₁ − 1/R₂)
n = refractive index of lens (in air)
Magnification
m = hᵢ/h₀ = v/u
Ratio of image height to object height
Plane Surface (R→∞)
n₂/v = n₁/u
Refraction at plane interface
Special Cases & Tips
Transparent Sphere (Object at Centre)
Apparent position = Actual position (image at v = −R), independent of refractive index
Bird in Air seen by Fish in Water
Apparent distance y = nx (n = R.I. of water). Bird appears farther and faster!
Object in Sphere formula
Object distance x = R/(n−1) for real image at same distance from second surface
Focal Length in Water
Focal length increases when lens is placed in water. Depends on surrounding medium.

Human Eye and Colourful World

Eye Structure · Defects of Vision · Prism · Dispersion · Scattering

Key Concepts
Eye Structure
  • Cornea: Transparent front cover of eye
  • Aqueous Humour: Liquid behind cornea
  • Iris: Coloured muscular diaphragm; controls pupil size
  • Pupil: Opening in iris; acts as variable aperture
  • Crystalline Lens: Responsible for image formation
  • Retina: Screen of eye; ~125 million rods & cones
  • Ciliary Muscles: Change focal length of eye lens
  • Optic Nerve: Transmits signals to brain (~1 million fibres)
  • Image distance (lens to retina) = 2.5 cm (fixed)
Vision Parameters
  • Least Distance of Distinct Vision: ~25 cm (healthy adult)
  • Angle of Vision: ~60° (maximum angle to see whole object)
  • Accommodation: Ability of eye lens to change focal length
  • f_max: 2.5 cm (object at ∞)
  • f_min: 2.27 cm (object at 25 cm)
  • Rods → detect intensity of light
  • Cones → identify colour
  • Eye lens forms a real & inverted image on retina
Defects of Vision
DefectProblemCorrection
MyopiaCannot see distant objects; f_max < 2.5 cm; image before retinaConcave (Bi-concave) lens
HypermetropiaCannot see near objects; f_min > 2.27 cm; image behind retinaConvex (Bi-convex) lens
PresbyopiaLoss of accommodation with age; both near & far affectedBi-focal lenses
Prism, Dispersion & Scattering
  • Prism: Transparent medium with two inclined plane surfaces
  • Angle of Prism (A): Angle between two refracting surfaces
  • Angle of Deviation (d): Angle between incident & emergent ray
  • Dispersion: Splitting of white light into VIBGYOR
  • Violet → most deviation; Red → least deviation
  • Refractive index decreases with increase in wavelength
  • Scattering: Re-emission of absorbed light in all directions
  • Blue sky → N₂ & O₂ molecules scatter blue light
  • Red sunrise/sunset → red scatters less, travels far
  • Rainbow → dispersion + total internal reflection in drops
Important Formulas
All Key Formulas
Max Focal Length (Object at ∞)
f_max = 2.5 cm
v = 2.5 cm, u = −∞ → 1/f = 1/2.5
Min Focal Length (Object at 25 cm)
f_min = 25/11 ≈ 2.27 cm
u = −25 cm, v = 2.5 cm
Myopia Correction (Concave Lens)
f = −D (D = far point distance)
u = −∞, v = −D
Hypermetropia Correction (Convex)
f = 25d/(25−d) cm
u = −25 cm, v = −d (near point)
Power of Lens
P = 1/f(m) = 100/f(cm) · Dioptre (D)
Reciprocal of focal length
Refractive Index of Prism
n = sin[(A+D)/2] / sin(A/2)
A = prism angle, D = min. deviation
Prism Geometry
A + d = i₁ + i₂ ; r₁ + r₂ = A
At minimum deviation: i₁ = i₂, r₁ = r₂ = A/2
Quick Tricks & Facts
Rainbow Angles
Red light: 42° · Violet light: 40° · Observer sees a 3D cone, not a flat arc
Sky Colour Facts
Blue sky (clear day) → N₂, O₂ scatter blue · White sky (hot day) → water vapour scatters all colours
Power Example
P = 2D → f = 100/2 = 50 cm (convex, positive power)
Raman Effect
C.V. Raman found: frequency of scattered light in liquids > incident light frequency
VIBGYOR Order
Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red · Violet = shortest λ, highest n

Structure of Atom

Wave Nature of Light · Bohr's Model · Quantum Numbers · Electronic Configuration

Key Concepts
Wave Nature of Light
  • Electromagnetic Wave: Oscillating electric & magnetic fields, perpendicular to each other and to direction of propagation
  • Speed of light c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
  • Relation: c = νλ (frequency × wavelength)
  • Visible spectrum: 400 nm (violet) to 700 nm (red)
  • Planck's Quantum Theory: Energy emitted in multiples of hν
  • E = hν (h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s)
  • Red → lower energy (lower ν) · Blue → higher energy (higher ν)
  • Line Spectra: Each element emits characteristic wavelengths ("fingerprints")
Atomic Models
  • Bohr's Model: Electrons in fixed stationary orbits; energy quantized (E₁, E₂, E₃...)
  • Electron absorbs energy → jumps to higher level (excited state)
  • Electron emits energy → falls to lower level; emission line visible
  • Limitation: Failed to explain fine (splitting) spectra
  • Bohr-Sommerfeld: Added elliptical orbits; explained fine spectra but failed for multi-electron atoms
  • Quantum Mechanical Model (Schrödinger): Electrons in orbitals (probability regions)
  • Orbital: Region of max probability of finding an electron
  • Position & velocity cannot be measured simultaneously (Uncertainty)
Quantum Numbers

Filling Principles
  • Pauli Exclusion: No two electrons in same atom can have all 4 QNs identical → max 2 electrons per orbital (with opposite spins)
  • Aufbau Principle: Fill lowest energy orbitals first. Order: 1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d …
  • Hund's Rule: All degenerate orbitals singly filled before pairing begins; unpaired electrons have parallel spins
  • Max electrons in shell = 2n²
  • Max electrons in subshell = 2(2l+1)
  • Subshells: s=2, p=6, d=10, f=14 electrons max

Orbital Shapes & Numbers
Number of Orbitals per Subshell (2l+1)
s (l=0): 1 orbital · p (l=1): 3 orbitals · d (l=2): 5 orbitals · f (l=3): 7 orbitals
Shapes
s = spherical · p = dumbbell (pₓ, p_y, p_z) · d = double dumbbell
Degenerate Orbitals
Orbitals in same subshell have same energy. E.g. all three 2p orbitals are degenerate.
Planck's Equation
E = hν · h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
Energy is quantized, not continuous
