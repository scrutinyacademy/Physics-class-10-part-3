Classification of Elements
The Periodic Table · Trends & Properties
⚗️ Key Formulas & Relations
Dobereiner's Triad
Atomic Wt (middle) = (A₁ + A₃) / 2
Middle element's atomic weight = average of first and third
Valency (Metals)
Valency = Group Number
For groups IA, IIA, IIIA (traditional notation)
Valency (Non-metals)
Valency = 8 − Group Number
For groups VA, VIA, VIIA elements
Atomic Weight Correction
At. Wt = Eq. Wt × Valency
Mendeleeff used to correct Be: 4.5 × 2 = 9
Electronegativity (Mulliken)
EN = (IE + EA) / 2
Average of Ionization Energy and Electron Affinity
Ionization Energy
M(g) + IE₁ → M⁺(g) + e⁻
Energy to remove outermost electron from neutral gas atom
Electron Affinity
M(g) + e⁻ → M⁻(g) + E₁
Energy released when electron added to neutral gaseous atom
Atomic Radius (SI)
1 pm = 10⁻¹² m
Measured in picometres; half of internuclear distance
📋 Revision Sheet — Key Points
  • Dobereiner (1829): Elements in triads — middle element's atomic weight = average of first & third
  • Newlands (1865): Law of Octaves — every 8th element has similar properties to first
  • Mendeleeff (1871): Periodic law based on atomic weight; predicted missing elements (eka-Al = Gallium)
  • Moseley (1913): Rearranged by atomic number; solved anomalous pairs like Te & I
  • Modern Periodic Law: Properties are periodic functions of electronic configuration
  • Modern table: 18 groups (vertical) × 7 periods (horizontal); 118+ elements
  • s-block (Groups 1–2): All metals; valence electrons in s-subshell
  • p-block (Groups 13–18): Metals, non-metals, metalloids; valence electrons in p-subshell
  • d-block (Groups 3–12): Transition metals; last electron in d-subshell
  • f-block: Inner transition elements — Lanthanoids (4f) and Actinoids (5f)
  • Atomic radius: Increases ↓ group; Decreases → period (more nuclear charge, same shell)
  • Ionization Energy: Decreases ↓ group; Generally increases → period
  • Electron Affinity: Decreases ↓ group; Increases → period
  • Electronegativity: F (4.0) is highest; Cs is least electronegative stable element
  • Metallic character: Increases ↓ group; Decreases → period
  • Cation is SMALLER than parent atom; Anion is LARGER than parent atom
  • Period 1: 2 elements (1s only); Period 2: 8 elements (2s + 2p)
  • Period 3: 8 elements; Period 4 & 5: 18 elements each; Period 6: 32 elements
  • Metalloids (staircase): B, Si, As, Ge — semi-conductors, brittle
  • Lanthanoids (57La–71Lu): 4f elements; Actinoids (90Th–103Lr): 5f elements

Chemical Bonding
Ionic · Covalent · VSEPR · Hybridisation

📋 Revision Sheet — Key Points
  • Chemical bond: force of attraction between atoms/group of atoms forming stable entity
  • Valence electrons (outermost shell) are responsible for bond formation
  • Noble gases (Group 18) are stable with 8 valence electrons — basis of octet rule
  • Octet Rule: atoms tend to achieve 8 electrons in outer shell (like noble gases)
  • Kossel & Lewis (1916): independently proposed electronic theory of valence
  • Ionic bond: transfer of electrons from metal atom to non-metal atom
  • Cation (positive ion): formed by loss of electrons from metal atoms
  • Anion (negative ion): formed by gain of electrons by non-metal atoms
  • Ionic compounds: crystalline solids, high MP, conduct electricity in solution
  • Covalent bond: mutual sharing of valence electrons between atoms
  • Single bond: 1 shared pair; Double bond: 2 shared pairs; Triple bond: 3 shared pairs
  • N₂ has triple bond; O₂ has double bond; F₂, H₂, Cl₂ have single bonds
  • Covalent compounds: low MP, low BP, soluble in non-polar solvents
  • VSEPR Theory: Sidgwick & Powell (1940), improved by Gillespie & Nyholm (1957)
  • Lone pairs occupy MORE space than bond pairs → distorts ideal bond angle
  • Valence Bond Theory: Linus Pauling (1954) — orbital overlap approach
  • σ (sigma) bond: head-on overlap along inter-nuclear axis — STRONGER
  • π (pi) bond: lateral/sideways overlap — WEAKER
  • Hybridisation: Linus Pauling (1931) — mixing of orbitals of similar energy
  • BeCl₂: sp hybrid (linear, 180°); BF₃: sp² hybrid (trigonal planar, 120°)
  • CH₄: sp³ hybrid (tetrahedral, 109°28′); NH₃: sp³ + lone pair (107°48′)
  • H₂O: sp³ + 2 lone pairs (bent/angular, 104°31′)
  • Polar covalent bond: unequal sharing due to electronegativity difference
  • Polar molecule: neutral but has partial +δ and −δ charges (e.g. HCl)
  • "Like dissolves in like" — ionic in polar solvents; covalent in non-polar solvents

Electric Current
Ohm's Law · Resistance · Power · Kirchhoff's Laws
⚡ Key Formulas
Electric Current
I = Q / t
Current (A) = Charge (C) ÷ Time (s)
Drift Velocity
I = nqAv_d
n = charge density, q = charge, A = area, v_d = drift speed
Ohm's Law
V = IR
Potential Difference = Current × Resistance
Resistance
R = ρl / A
ρ = resistivity, l = length, A = cross-section area
Series Resistance
R_eq = R₁ + R₂ + R₃
Resistances simply add in series
Parallel Resistance
1/R_eq = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃
Reciprocals add in parallel
Electric Power
P = VI = I²R = V²/R
Power in Watts; three equivalent forms
Electrical Energy
W = Pt = VIt
Energy in Joules (Ws) or kWh
Units Conversion
1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
1 kW × 1 hr = 1 unit of electricity
EMF Definition
ε = W/q
Work done by chemical force per unit charge (battery)
Potential Difference
V = W/q = F_e · l / q
Work per unit charge; unit = Volt = J/C
Parallel (2 resistors)
R_eq = R₁R₂ / (R₁+R₂)
Product over sum formula for two parallel resistors
📋 Revision Sheet — Key Points
  • Electric current = ordered motion of charges (electrons drift toward +ve terminal)
  • 1 Coulomb = 6.625 × 10¹⁸ electrons; 1 Ampere = 1 C/s
  • Ammeter: connected in series; Voltmeter: connected in parallel
  • Drude-Lorentz model: free electrons move randomly; battery gives directed drift
  • Drift speed of electrons in copper ≈ 7×10⁻⁵ m/s (very slow!)
  • Direction of current = direction of +ve charge flow = OPPOSITE to electron flow
  • 1 Volt = 1 Joule/Coulomb; 1 Ohm = 1 Volt/Ampere
  • Ohm's Law: V ∝ I (at constant temperature) → V/I = R = constant
  • Ohmic materials: metals (V-I graph is straight line through origin)
  • Non-ohmic materials: LEDs, semiconductors (curved V-I graph)
  • Ohm's Law NOT valid for: gaseous conductors, semiconductors (Ge, Si)
  • Resistance increases with temperature (filament bulb resistance is higher when hot)
  • Resistivity ρ depends on material & temperature only (NOT geometry)
  • Conductivity σ = 1/ρ; Silver has lowest resistivity (best conductor)
  • Tungsten used in bulb filament: high resistivity + high melting point (3422°C)
  • Nichrome & Manganin: 30-100× more resistive than metals; used in heaters
  • Semiconductors: Si, Ge — resistivity 10⁵–10¹⁰ × that of metals
  • Series circuit: same current through all; voltages add up
  • Parallel circuit: same voltage across all; currents add up
  • Kirchhoff's Junction Law: ΣI(in) = ΣI(out) — based on conservation of charge
  • Kirchhoff's Loop Law: ΣV = 0 around any closed loop — based on conservation of energy
  • Electric shock: depends on current (I), voltage (V) and body resistance
  • Fatal current: 0.07 A through heart for > 1 second can be fatal
  • Bird on wire is safe: no potential difference between its legs (single wire)
  • Household wiring: parallel connection; fuse protects against overloading
  • Fuse: thin wire with low melting point; melts when current exceeds safe limit
  • 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J = 1 "unit" of electricity on your bill
  • EMF: work done by chemical force per unit charge from −ve to +ve terminal inside battery
  • R ∝ l (length) and R ∝ 1/A (area) at constant temperature
  • Equivalent resistance of parallel < any individual resistance in the combination
